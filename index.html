Activity 01 · Notice

AI in the Room?

Choose one object or service nearby. Your job is not to prove it uses AI. Your job is to investigate carefully.

Your mission Decide whether the evidence really supports the claim that AI is involved.

You’ll leave knowing “Smart” is a label. Good investigators trace inputs, outputs, decisions, and uncertainty.

1

Choose your mystery

A phone camera, game, streaming app, speaker, watch, search box, toy, or something else.

2

Follow the clues

Select every clue you can actually observe.

3

Map the system

Why this matters outside the activity

“Uses AI” can change what people buy, trust, or allow.

Products, schools, employers, and public services may describe systems as intelligent or AI-powered. Following the inputs, outputs, decisions, and evidence helps you ask what the label actually means.

Takeaway in action: Before trusting the label, ask what evidence would confirm—or challenge—it.

Pip’s field note: “I don’t know yet” is not a failed answer. It is an honest place to begin an investigation.

Activity 02 · Test

The Three Mystery Boxes

Each box behaves differently. Try inputs, observe outputs, and decide what kind of box it might be.

Your mission Design tests that reveal how each box behaves—not just tests it can easily pass.

You’ll leave knowing Rules, predictions, and generated responses can look similar until you probe their limits.

Try a number from 1 to 20.

Box B

Waiting for a test…
What is your best explanation?

Why this matters outside the activity

Important systems need tougher tests than their best examples.

A recommendation, detector, or generated answer may look impressive on familiar inputs and fail on unusual people, conditions, languages, or situations.

Takeaway in action: Test the edge, the exception, and the uncertain case—not only the demo that works.

Try a stronger test: What input could reveal the edge of each box’s abilities? Good investigators test more than the happy path.

Activity 03 · Question

The Data Backpack

Imagine an AI that identifies neighborhood birds. Its “backpack” contains the examples it learned from.

Your mission Repack the examples so the bird identifier has a fairer, safer view of the world.

You’ll leave knowing Data quality includes what is missing, repeated, private, outdated, and collected without enough context.

Tap the cards

What do you notice?

The number and variety of examples can shape what an AI notices—and what it misses.

Pack a better backpack

Which improvements matter? Choose at least three.

Why this matters outside the activity

What gets collected can shape who a system notices.

Training data can influence whether a tool works across seasons, neighborhoods, bodies, accents, devices, or environments—and whether private information is exposed along the way.

Takeaway in action: Ask who and what is missing, overrepresented, outdated, or included without enough permission.

Remember: More data is not automatically better data. Ask whether it is relevant, varied, accurate, respectful, and collected with permission.

Why this matters outside the activity

Charts influence attention before anyone reads the fine print.

News, social posts, school reports, public dashboards, and product summaries use framing choices that can clarify a pattern—or make it look more dramatic than the evidence supports.

Takeaway in action: Separate observation from explanation, inspect the scale, and ask what was not measured.

Pip’s data note: Use made-up numbers or information that is safe to share. A fun visualization never requires your full name, location, school, or private habits.
