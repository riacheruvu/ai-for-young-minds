# Partner and Resource-Listing Notes

Last reviewed: June 14, 2026

## Short description

**AI Field Guide for Young Minds** is a free, privacy-preserving collection of interactive activities that helps young learners investigate how AI systems work, question data and model limitations, make evidence-based claims, and choose how AI participates in their learning.

It requires no account, collects no information, uses no external AI service, and can be completed online or adapted for offline discussion.

## Why it is different

The guide combines:

- Child-accessible investigations rather than definitions alone
- Real AI-system and trustworthy-AI thinking
- Data literacy and model limitation exercises
- Explicit learner agency
- Optional bridges into coding and emerging technology
- Honest treatment of uncertainty

It was created by Ria Cheruvu, a trustworthy AI architect and technical educator who began working in technology at age 14 and has taught AI, data science, AI ethics, and machine-learning deployment across professional and public settings.

## Provisional audience

The current pilot is likely most suitable for:

- Upper-elementary and middle-school learners with adult facilitation
- Older learners working independently
- Libraries, STEM clubs, family-learning programs, and introductory classrooms

This age positioning is provisional. Formal grade-band validation and classroom outcome studies have not yet been completed.

## Resource characteristics

- Cost: Free
- Account: None
- Data collection: None
- Advertising: None
- External dependencies: None
- AI tool required: No
- Format: Static interactive website
- Accessibility: Keyboard-accessible controls, semantic labels, responsive design, reduced-motion support
- Adaptability: Activities can be spoken, drawn, projected, printed, or role-played

## Learning experiences

| Experience | Learner action | Main takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| AI in the Room? | Investigate evidence of AI in an everyday system | Trace evidence before trusting an AI label |
| Three Mystery Boxes | Design tests for rules, predictions, and generation | Test limits, not only successful behavior |
| Data Backpack | Inspect missing, repeated, narrow, and private examples | Training data shapes what a system can notice |
| AI Curiosity Plan | Choose when and how AI helps | Learners retain agency over their process |
| My Mini Wrapped | Build and critique a tiny data story | Data presentation involves human choices |
| Seven Tiny Systems Labs | Predict how seven emerging-technology systems respond to a change | Complex technologies become understandable through their rules, representations, limits, and consequences |

## Alignment

The guide supports several current AI-literacy priorities:

- Foundational AI literacy and critical thinking
- Curiosity, creativity, and active participation
- Understanding AI capabilities and limitations
- Data literacy
- Societal impact and responsible participation
- Human agency and informed interaction with AI

Potential AI4K12 connections include:

- Big Idea 2: Representation and Reasoning
- Big Idea 3: Learning
- Big Idea 5: Societal Impact

The resource also reflects the April 23, 2025 U.S. executive order’s emphasis on early AI exposure, foundational AI literacy, critical thinking, curiosity, creativity, and opportunities for students to use and create with AI.

## Suggested listing copy

> A free, no-login interactive field guide that helps young learners test AI behavior, investigate training data, question model limitations, create data stories, and make deliberate choices about using AI. Designed for flexible use in classrooms, libraries, clubs, and family conversations.

## Submission priorities

### 1. AI4K12 Resource Directory

Best topical fit. AI4K12 maintains a curated K–12 AI resource list and provides a public resource-suggestion form.

- Site: https://ai4k12.org/
- Resource form: linked from the Resources section of the AI4K12 homepage

Before submission:

- Add provisional grade bands to each activity.
- Create one printable facilitator sheet.
- Map each activity to relevant AI4K12 progression-chart concepts.

### 2. OER Commons

Best for educator discovery and reuse.

- Site: https://oercommons.org/

Important licensing decision: the current CC BY-NC license limits some reuse and may not satisfy stricter definitions of fully open educational resources. For maximum adoption, consider CC BY 4.0 or CC BY-SA 4.0.

Before submission:

- Decide on the final Creative Commons license.
- Add downloadable/printable activity versions.
- Include audience, duration, objectives, and accessibility metadata.

### 3. Local and State 4-H / Cooperative Extension

Best practical pilot channel. The 2025 federal AI-education order specifically identifies 4-H and the Cooperative Extension System as channels for formal and non-formal AI education.

- Find local 4-H: https://4-h.org/about/find/

Pitch a small facilitated pilot, not a national partnership first:

- One 45–60 minute workshop
- AI in the Room?
- Data Backpack
- Small Experiments, Big Ideas
- Short learner and facilitator feedback

### 4. CSTA and Computer-Science Educator Communities

Best channel for educator feedback, workshops, and eventual conference proposals.

- CSTA: https://csteachers.org/
- TeachAI: https://www.teachai.org/

Position the resource as an open classroom pilot and invite structured educator feedback. Do not claim standards alignment before completing the mapping.

### 5. Public Libraries, Science Museums, and Youth STEM Programs

Best channel for direct use with children.

Start locally with a ready-to-run event package:

- 45-minute session plan
- Projector-friendly website
- Printable Curiosity Plan
- No-login privacy statement
- Feedback card for learners

## Federal challenge note

The first Presidential AI Challenge cycle concluded in June 2026. The resource itself is better positioned as preparation or facilitation material for future student challenges than as a challenge entry.

## Recommended next evidence step

Run three small pilots:

1. A family or friend group
2. A library, club, or informal youth program
3. A classroom or educator-led group

Capture:

- Which activity learners choose
- Where they ask new questions
- Where language is confusing
- Whether they revise an initial claim
- What adults need to facilitate well

This evidence will make directory submissions and partnership conversations much stronger.
