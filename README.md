# AI Field Guide for Young Minds

A playful, privacy-preserving set of activities for discovering how AI works, asking good questions, and testing its limits.

**[Open the field guide](https://riacheruvu.github.io/ai-for-young-minds/)**

> This is a small, evolving public resource—not a complete curriculum or a claim to have solved AI education.

## Why I made this

I have spent years creating courses, technical learning materials, talks, and early book drafts about AI, machine learning, data, and responsible technology.

Some of my favorite ideas began with simple questions:

- What goes into the “funny box”?
- What comes out?
- How could we test it?
- What did the system miss?
- What should remain ours?

AI has changed considerably since I first drafted some of those materials. The need to ask good questions has not.

I built this field guide to carefully reuse and update the parts that still feel valuable: concrete experiments, systems thinking, data literacy, honest uncertainty, and learner agency. It reflects my experience both building high-stakes AI systems and trying to explain technical ideas without making people feel small.

## What is inside

Four short field stops:

1. **AI in the Room?** — trace evidence before trusting an AI label
2. **The Three Mystery Boxes** — test rules, predictions, and generated responses
3. **The Data Backpack** — investigate what training examples include and leave out
4. **My AI Curiosity Plan** — choose where AI joins your learning process

Two optional project trails:

- **My Mini Wrapped** — create a tiny data story, then challenge its framing
- **Seven Tiny Systems Labs** — explore digital twins, 3D meshes, quantum circuits, linked ledgers, spiking neurons, flocking, and computing energy

Technical ideas and math are available as optional deeper layers. No learner has to complete everything.

## What this is—and is not

This resource is:

- Free and usable without an account
- Designed to work without an AI tool
- Interactive, discussion-friendly, and adaptable
- Privacy-preserving by design
- A starting point for investigation rather than a catalog of answers

This resource is not:

- A comprehensive AI curriculum
- Formally validated for a specific grade band
- A replacement for an educator, caregiver, or subject-matter expert
- A guide to whichever AI product happens to be popular this month

The suggested fit is approximately grades 5–9, with room to simplify or go deeper. That range is provisional.

## Current status

This is an early public release.

The activities have been tested for responsive layout, accessibility basics, and interaction behavior. They have **not** yet been evaluated through a formal classroom study. Improvements should come from observed use and specific feedback—not assumptions or inflated claims.

See [`ROADMAP.md`](ROADMAP.md) for the intentionally small update plan.

## Design principles

- Treat young people as capable investigators
- Begin with curiosity, not vocabulary
- Ask for evidence before confidence
- Make uncertainty a valid result
- Keep technical depth optional but real
- Collect no personal information
- Preserve meaningful human ownership
- Remove outdated material instead of accumulating it

## Use and adapt it

Choose one activity. Use it independently, in conversation, or with a group. Speak, draw, print, project, or role-play instead of clicking when that works better.

The website is a static GitHub Pages project:

```powershell
python -m http.server 8000
```

Then open `http://localhost:8000`.
